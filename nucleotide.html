Nucleotide

What is a Nucleotide?

A nucleotide is the basic building block of DNA and RNA. Nucleotides join together to form long chains that store and transmit genetic information. The sequence of nucleotides determines the genetic instructions used by living organisms.

Structure

Every nucleotide contains three main components:

  • A phosphate group
  • A five-carbon sugar
  • A nitrogenous base

In DNA, the sugar is deoxyribose. In RNA, the sugar is ribose.

Nitrogenous Bases

DNA Bases

  • Adenine (A)
  • Thymine (T)
  • Cytosine (C)
  • Guanine (G)

RNA Bases

  • Adenine (A)
  • Uracil (U)
  • Cytosine (C)
  • Guanine (G)

How Nucleotides Form DNA

Nucleotides connect through phosphodiester bonds to form the sugar-phosphate backbone of DNA and RNA. The order of their bases stores genetic information.

Functions

  • Store genetic information
  • Form DNA and RNA molecules
  • Participate in cellular communication
  • Help transfer energy within cells

Nucleotides Beyond DNA

Some nucleotides have important roles outside of genetics. For example, ATP (adenosine triphosphate) is a nucleotide that serves as the primary energy currency of cells.

Interesting Facts

  • Nucleotides are the smallest units of genetic information.
  • Human DNA contains billions of nucleotides.
  • ATP, the cell's main energy molecule, is a nucleotide.
  • DNA and RNA differ in both their sugar and one of their bases.
